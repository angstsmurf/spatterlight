Garden
The garden in front of the hotel is quite big. It gently slopes down towards the south from the porch, ending abruptly on the edge of a steep cliff. A white picket fence has been erected a few yards from the edge, preventing careless guests from wandering too close to the vertical drop.

A few trees, quite young, yet standing with a full canopy, provide shelter from the sun and a few guests are sitting around tables underneath them enjoying the incredible view over the Mexican Gulf. An old crooked tree stands just on the other side of the fence, leaning dangerously over the edge of the cliff. Its naked branches reach towards the sky, except one that is reaching out horizontally from the gnarled trunk.

Close to the foot of the tree there are two old weatherbeaten headstones leaning slightly to one side.

To the south lies the hotel, looking peaceful in the summer sun.

 *House of Ill Repute Turns Decent*
The house known as Lulu-Belle's Safe Harbor, a notorious brothel in the days when pirates prowled the Gulf, has perhaps finally settled. Rumors have long existed of a curse upon the Safe Harbor’s tower room, which forced the place to close shortly after the death of Lulu-Belle in 1700. Since then, it has served as an unofficial historical landmark, but no longer provided services for paying travellers.

In the years since the house stood empty, it has been an informal shelter for the homeless and even the famous smuggler Hautaín, not to mention the numerous other treasure hunters who almost tore the house down in search of treasure. Captain LeBeuf, the famous pirate, is said to have left his “treasure and his heart” at the Safe Harbor some time before his death, the same year as Belle’s.

Only recently has Belle’s Harbor, slowly been given a renaissance since Belle's great, great granddaughter, Susan LeBeuf, bought the place and turned it into a respectable hotel just days before it was to be torn down. A new crowd of tourists, drawn by the legends around the historical building, has sought their safe harbor at the Safe Harbor.

Although originally in bad repair, Miss Susan has managed to bring the Safe Harbor back to its former beauty, modeling all of the renovation after styles of the late 17th century with just a few modern touches. Only the tower room, cursed according to a local legend, remains locked. Though Susan reports she holds no superstition about the tower room, she claims to keep it locked out of respect of her great, great grandmother, Lulu-Belle.

“Keeping the legend alive is important,” says Susan. “It’s an important part of our community’s history and culture as a colonial port town. Besides, it’s good for business."
